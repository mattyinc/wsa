# What Makes Good Money?

## Subtitle

**Why gold, shells, cigarettes, and Bitcoin teach us what money really is**

---

# Big Question

Why didn't humanity use bananas as money?

---

# Opening Story

Imagine you own a bakery.

A customer wants to buy bread.

Instead of paying with cash, they offer you:

* a chicken
* a bag of rice
* a gold coin
* ten bananas

Which payment would you prefer?

More importantly...

Why?

---

# Why This Matters

Not everything can become money.

To support a modern economy, money must have certain characteristics.

Without them, trade becomes unreliable and inefficient.

---

# Plain-English Explanation

Good money should be:

## Durable

It shouldn't rot or decay.

## Portable

It should be easy to carry.

## Divisible

You should be able to make smaller payments.

## Uniform

Every unit should be roughly the same.

## Scarce

It shouldn't be easy to create unlimited amounts.

## Widely Accepted

People must believe others will accept it too.

These characteristics explain why some forms of money survived while others disappeared.

---

# Mental Model

## Money Is A Tool

A good hammer drives nails efficiently.

A good currency moves value efficiently.

The better the tool, the easier life becomes.

---

# Wall Street Perspective

Professionals constantly evaluate whether a currency is becoming stronger or weaker.

They examine:

* trust
* inflation
* scarcity
* liquidity
* government credibility
* monetary policy

Money is never just paper.

It is part of an economic system.

---

# Historical Case Study

## Rai Stones

The people of Yap once used enormous stone disks as money.

They worked because everyone agreed on ownership.

But they were difficult to move.

This demonstrates why portability matters.

---

# Modern Example

Compare the following using today's lesson:

Which characteristics does each possess?

Which are weaker?

# Common Beginner Mistakes

* Thinking all currencies are equally trustworthy.
* Assuming physical money is always better than digital money.
* Ignoring scarcity.
* Confusing popularity with usefulness.
