# Putting It All Together

## Subtitle

**Following one dollar through the global financial system**

---

# Big Question

You've spent the last nine lessons learning what money is, how banks create it, why interest exists, how inflation changes it, why liquidity matters, and what central banks actually do.

Now it's time to answer one final question.

**How does all of this fit together?**

Today, we're going to follow a single dollar from the moment it is created until it disappears.

By the end of this lesson, you'll no longer see money as cash.

You'll see a living system.

---

# Opening Story

Imagine a young engineer named Daniel.

He has a dream.

He wants to build a company that manufactures solar panels.

The problem?

He needs money.

Not because he's lazy.

Not because he lacks ideas.

Because building a factory requires resources before revenue arrives.

Daniel visits a commercial bank.

The bank approves a business loan.

At that moment...

new purchasing power enters the economy.

The money didn't exist in Daniel's account yesterday.

Today it does.

His journey begins.

---

# Step One

## Credit Creates Purchasing Power

The bank records:

Asset:

Business Loan

Liability:

Daniel's Deposit

Daniel now pays:

- architects
- engineers
- construction workers
- equipment suppliers

Those people deposit their income into their own banks.

The money begins moving.

One loan...

dozens of transactions.

Immediately.

---

# Step Two

## Production Begins

Construction finishes.

Machines arrive.

Employees are hired.

Raw materials are purchased.

Electricity is connected.

Months later...

the factory begins producing solar panels.

Notice something important.

The loan itself didn't create wealth.

The factory did.

Credit financed the creation of productive assets.

This is the difference between productive borrowing and speculative borrowing.

---

# Step Three

## Income Circulates

Workers receive salaries.

Employees buy groceries.

Restaurants receive customers.

Landlords receive rent.

Farmers receive payments.

Truck drivers deliver products.

Every transaction becomes someone else's income.

Money keeps moving.

The economy behaves less like a collection of individuals...

and more like one enormous network.

---

# Step Four

## Savings Return To The Banking System

Many people spend only part of their income.

The remainder becomes savings.

Those savings remain inside commercial banks.

Banks now possess stronger balance sheets.

They evaluate new lending opportunities.

Another entrepreneur appears.

Another loan is approved.

The cycle begins again.

Notice something.

Money rarely sits still.

It flows.

Constantly.

---

# Mental Model

## The Economy Is A Circulatory System

Think about the human body.

Blood transports oxygen.

Without circulation...

organs fail.

The economy works similarly.

Money transports purchasing power.

Banks act like arteries.

Businesses become muscles.

Consumers become cells.

Central banks help regulate pressure.

If circulation slows dramatically...

the entire system suffers.

Money isn't valuable because it exists.

Money is valuable because it moves.

---

# Step Five

## Inflation Appears

Suppose demand for solar panels explodes.

Factories cannot produce enough.

Customers compete.

Prices rise.

Meanwhile...

the factory needs more workers.

Wages increase.

Suppliers increase prices.

Inflation begins to emerge.

Notice something.

Inflation wasn't caused by a single event.

It emerged from interactions throughout the economy.

---

# Step Six

## The Central Bank Responds

The central bank observes:

- inflation rising
- borrowing accelerating
- demand remaining strong

Officials decide to raise interest rates.

Commercial banks increase lending rates.

Businesses become more selective.

Consumers delay purchases.

Economic growth slows slightly.

Inflation gradually moderates.

Again...

notice the sequence.

The central bank never ordered businesses to slow down.

It changed incentives.

Millions of people adjusted their own decisions.

---

# Step Seven

## The Loan Is Repaid

Years later...

Daniel's business succeeds.

Profits increase.

Each month he repays part of the loan.

As principal repayments occur...

the bank's balance sheet shrinks.

The purchasing power originally created by the loan gradually disappears.

Remember Lesson Five.

Loans create deposits.

Loan repayments destroy deposits.

Money enters.

Money leaves.

The cycle continues.

# Wall Street Perspective

This entire lesson can be summarized by one observation.

Professionals rarely analyze one company in isolation.

They ask:

- Where is credit expanding?
- Where is liquidity flowing?
- What are central banks doing?
- How expensive is money?
- How confident are consumers?
- How are businesses responding?

Markets are systems.

Everything influences everything else.

---

# Historical Perspective

Throughout history...

financial crises have looked different.

Some involved gold.

Some involved banks.

Some involved governments.

Some involved housing.

Some involved technology.

Yet almost every major crisis contained the same ingredients.

- excessive optimism
- excessive borrowing
- deteriorating confidence
- tightening liquidity

The details change.

Human behavior rarely does.

---

# A New Way To Read The News

Suppose tomorrow you read the following headline.

> "Central Bank Raises Interest Rates By 0.5%"

Six months ago...

you may have ignored it.

Now your brain asks different questions.

- Why?
- Is inflation rising?
- Will businesses borrow less?
- How will housing respond?
- Which companies benefit?
- Which industries struggle?
- What happens to bonds?
- What happens to the currency?

Congratulations.

You're beginning to think like an analyst.

---

# Common Beginner Mistakes

- Looking at markets one headline at a time.
- Separating banking from investing.
- Ignoring incentives.
- Ignoring expectations.
- Thinking money itself creates wealth.
- Assuming stock prices move randomly.

If you remember only one lesson from this course...

let it be this.
